Technology

Reader, optics, cartridge, and self-calibration as one system.

Sepsensor is a fully automated optical immunosensor. A compact reusable reader, a disposable microfluidic cartridge, and a proprietary self-calibration method combine to deliver quantitative IL-6 concentration at the bedside — in 10 minutes, without specialist training.

System architecture

Three components designed as one bedside instrument.

Each component of Sepsensor is designed to function as part of a single clinical workflow. The reader, cartridge, and assay are co-designed — not assembled from off-the-shelf parts — to ensure the system works reliably in a bedside environment.

Component 1

Reusable compact reader

The reader houses the optical detection system — camera module, optical filter set, illumination source, and embedded processor. It is designed to be compact enough for bedside use and durable enough for repeated clinical deployment. A single reader services multiple patients through its compatible disposable cartridges.

Component 2

Disposable microfluidic cartridge

Each single-use cartridge contains the detection surfaces, reagents, and sample preparation channels. The operator loads a small plasma sample, inserts the cartridge into the reader, and the assay runs automatically. No pipetting, no calibration steps, no specialist knowledge required. The consumable model creates a clear recurring revenue stream alongside the hardware.

Component 3

Proprietary immunoassay

The assay chemistry is Sepsensor's core IP. It is designed to deliver quantitative IL-6 measurement with built-in self-calibration — enabling reliable, repeatable results at the bedside without the need for external calibrators or laboratory-grade quality control infrastructure.

The key innovation

Self-calibration is what makes bedside accuracy possible.

Every optical biosensor faces the same fundamental challenge: the signal you measure is affected by variation between individual cartridges — differences in surface coating, reagent concentration, and optical alignment that shift the fluorescence reading independently of the actual IL-6 concentration in the sample. Without correction, this makes bedside quantification unreliable.

Sepsensor's proprietary self-calibration method solves this problem at the chemistry level — not as a post-hoc software correction, but integrated into the assay design itself. The result: every cartridge produces accurate, traceable IL-6 measurements without requiring a trained operator, external reference standards, or batch calibration runs. This is the technical enabler that makes a point-of-care instrument clinically credible — and it is the capability that conventional lateral flow approaches fundamentally cannot replicate.

No external calibration required

Each cartridge is self-referencing. Clinicians and nurses can operate the device without any calibration procedure — insert, run, read. This is critical for bedside deployment where lab-grade QC infrastructure is unavailable.

Consistent results across manufacturing batches

The calibration method corrects for cartridge-to-cartridge variation introduced during manufacturing — a major quality control challenge for all disposable diagnostic consumables. Sepsensor addresses this structurally rather than relying on tight manufacturing tolerances.

Platform scalability

IL-6 is the first assay. The platform is built for more.

Sepsensor is not a single-test device. The reader hardware, optical architecture, and self-calibration method are designed to support multiple simultaneous biomarker assays from a single cartridge run. Starting with IL-6 establishes the platform credibility and regulatory foundation — then the product roadmap expands.

Phase 1 — Now

IL-6 for early sepsis detection

The first validated assay measures IL-6 — the earliest rising sepsis biomarker — with a 10-minute bedside result. This establishes the clinical use case, the regulatory pathway, and the commercial proof point that enables everything that follows.

Phase 2 — Near-term

Multi-biomarker sepsis panel

The platform is designed to add procalcitonin (PCT) and C-reactive protein (CRP) as additional assay channels on the same cartridge. A combined IL-6 / PCT / CRP panel from a single bedside run would represent the most comprehensive point-of-care sepsis inflammatory assessment currently possible.

Phase 3 — Future

AI-assisted interpretation and beyond sepsis

Trend data from continuous monitoring — with IL-6 results every 10 minutes — creates a longitudinal dataset that is ideal for AI-assisted treatment response scoring. Beyond sepsis, the platform architecture is applicable to any condition requiring rapid, repeated bedside inflammatory biomarker monitoring.

Optical detection

Camera-based fluorescence for portable, high-sensitivity detection.

The reader uses a compact camera module and optical filter set to capture fluorescence emission from the assay cartridge. Image processing algorithms running on the embedded processor convert raw fluorescence data to a quantitative IL-6 concentration in real time — displaying the result on-screen at the bedside within 10 minutes of sample loading.

Compact and portable form factor

The camera-based approach enables a reader that is small enough to sit on a bedside trolley or be carried to the patient, unlike benchtop fluorescence instruments which require fixed laboratory infrastructure.

Real-time image processing pipeline

Custom algorithms process the fluorescence image captured from the cartridge, extract the relevant signal intensities, apply the self-calibration correction, and output a quantitative result — all within the 10-minute assay run time.

The self-calibration architecture is Sepsensor's primary technical differentiator. It is not found in any current commercial bedside IL-6 product. IP protection activities are ongoing — measurement data is being collected to support patent filings covering the calibration method.

IVDR-aligned design from the start.

The product has been designed with awareness of the EU In Vitro Diagnostic Regulation (IVDR) pathway. A formal regulatory roadmap has been developed in collaboration with advisors as part of the STAC programme, covering the route from prototype to CE-marked clinical device.
