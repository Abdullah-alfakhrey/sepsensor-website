Technology

Reader, cartridge, self-calibration.

Built around a stable optical read.

  • Camera + optical filters. Fluorescence is captured with a compact optical system.
  • Signal processing. Software translates the optical read into the assay output.
  • Disposable microfluidic cartridge. Detection surfaces and sample-handling steps stay inside the strip.
  • Ratio-based self-calibration. The assay compares detection and reference channels to manage variation.

Reader

Portable optical core.

  • Camera-led detection. The reader captures the fluorescence image for software analysis.
  • Compact form factor. The public-facing concept is clearly positioned for bedside use.

Cartridge

Disposable path. Reusable hardware.

  • Microfluidic workflow. The cartridge contains detection surfaces and sample operations.
  • Bedside handling. Single-use loading keeps the reader itself out of the consumable loop.

Signal control

Self-calibration is the differentiator.

  • Reference signal inside the assay. The prototype direction uses a built-in calibration channel.
  • Designed to reduce drift. Cartridge and measurement variation are part of the problem being addressed.

Stage note

Prototype visuals. Prototype language.

  • No finished-device claim. This is a current development direction, not a commercial launch state.
  • No public lab dataset here. The page focuses on architecture and workflow fit.

What to remember

One workflow. Four memorable pieces.

  • IL-6 assay first. The cartridge and software are aligned around a specific first use case.
  • Optical readout. Camera, filters, and fluorescence stay at the heart of the design.
  • Self-calibration layer. Signal stability is part of the platform story, not an afterthought.
  • Bedside intent. Everything is shaped around near-patient use, not central-lab batching.
