Why now

Timing changes the value of the result.

Sepsensor is being designed around the idea that assay quality only matters if the result arrives while action is still useful. Bedside placement is therefore part of the product definition, not just a deployment detail.

Lab delay reduces decision speed Near-patient use is the design lens Prototype target under 30 minutes
Why this matters

The device is a workflow intervention as much as a sensing platform.

That framing helps clinicians, collaborators, and partners understand why turnaround time sits at the center of the product thesis.

Clinical rationale

Sepsensor is positioned around speed with a purpose.

The argument is not simply that faster is better. It is that earlier signal can keep the decision window open for treatment, triage, and monitoring.

Today

Distance from the bedside creates friction.

Lab-centered workflows can insert delay and handoffs between sampling, processing, and interpretation.

Target

Bring the readout closer to the moment of care.

The prototype is designed around a near-patient workflow where result visibility is faster and easier to act on.

Implication

The product story becomes clearer for partners.

A strong timing narrative makes the device easier to evaluate as a real clinical tool rather than a general assay experiment.

Bedside workflow

A timing-led product makes every component easier to justify.

When the thesis is clear, the form factor, interface, cartridge interaction, and assay focus all make more sense as parts of the same workflow.

Interface

The results screen is part of the clinical story because it shortens the gap between readout and interpretation.

Reader form

The device needs to feel like near-patient equipment rather than a lab-side instrument.

Prototype discipline

Every design choice is easier to defend when speed and bedside relevance stay central.

Prototype in validation.

Sepsensor is not a commercially approved medical device.
