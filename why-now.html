Why now

Every hour of delay narrows the window.

Sepsis is a medical emergency measured in minutes and hours, not days. Yet the diagnostic infrastructure supporting it — central lab IL-6 testing — operates on a 12-hour minimum turnaround. That gap is the problem Sepsensor is built to close.

12 hrs → 10 min Bedside decision support 48,000 UK deaths per year
Clinical urgency

The disease doesn't wait for the lab.

For every hour sepsis goes untreated, the risk of organ failure escalates. Faster inflammatory biomarker detection at the bedside changes the antibiotic decision window from hours to minutes.

The diagnostic gap

The result arrives after the treatment window has closed.

Sepsis progresses rapidly — inflammatory cytokines like IL-6 rise within the first few hours of infection onset, and the window for effective antibiotic intervention is measured in hours, not days. Yet central laboratory IL-6 testing has a minimum turnaround of 12 hours. That mismatch is the clinical problem Sepsensor is designed to close.

The biology

IL-6 rises within hours of infection.

Interleukin-6 is one of the earliest cytokines released in response to bacterial infection. Elevated IL-6 levels are detectable before clinical symptoms become obvious — making it a uniquely valuable early warning signal for sepsis.

The infrastructure

Lab processing takes 12 hours minimum.

Current IL-6 measurement requires a blood sample to be collected, transported to a central laboratory, queued in a batch process, and analysed with specialist equipment. Best-case turnaround is 12 hours. In many hospitals it is longer.

The consequence

Treatment is delayed at the worst moment.

When IL-6 results arrive hours after collection, clinicians face uncertain decisions about antibiotic escalation and fluid resuscitation. The cost of waiting — in mortality, ICU length of stay, and NHS resource — is measurable and significant.

Bedside placement

The device needs to be where the decision is made.

Moving IL-6 measurement to the bedside isn't just a logistics improvement — it changes the entire clinical interaction. Results arrive in the same timeframe as taking observations, before the patient leaves the ward, before a treatment decision must be made without data.

Decisions happen at the bedside, not in the lab

Clinicians need inflammatory biomarker data while they are still with the patient — not hours later via a lab results portal. Bedside placement closes the loop between measurement and action.

Continuous monitoring changes the treatment model

A bedside device enables repeat measurements every 10 minutes — something impossible with central lab testing. Trend data allows antibiotic response to be tracked in real time, not guessed retrospectively.

Resource-limited settings need decentralised diagnostics

Rural hospitals, emergency departments in developing countries, and resource-constrained NHS trusts cannot rely on centralised lab infrastructure. Bedside IL-6 measurement brings high-sensitivity diagnostics to settings where it has never been available.

Market timing

The convergence that makes this possible now.

Three trends are converging to make bedside fluorescence-based IL-6 sensing viable for the first time: miniaturised camera optics, low-cost microfluidics, and validated self-calibration chemistry. Sepsensor combines all three into a single integrated system.

Optics

Camera sensors are now precise enough.

Compact, high-resolution camera modules — driven by smartphone hardware advances — now provide the optical sensitivity needed for fluorescence immunoassay detection outside a laboratory setting.

Microfluidics

Cartridge manufacturing is accessible.

Advances in microfluidic fabrication have reduced the cost and complexity of producing disposable diagnostic cartridges — making the consumable model that drives recurring revenue commercially viable.

The global sepsis diagnostics market was valued at $596.6M in 2021 and is projected to grow at ~8% CAGR through 2027, driven by NHS pressure, ICU expansion, and increasing demand for near-patient testing.
